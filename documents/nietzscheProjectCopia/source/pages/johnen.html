EITCA DO JOHN

John Stuart Mill, a utilitarian philosopher of the 19th century, developed an ethics centered on the principle of utility, also known as the greatest happiness principle. Here is a summary of Mill's ethics: Utility Principle: Mill argues that the morality of actions should be judged based on their ability to promote the greatest happiness or pleasure and minimize pain or suffering. Happiness is understood as pleasure and the absence of pain. Consequentialism: Mill's ethics is consequentialist, which means that actions are evaluated by their results. The morality of an action depends on its consequences, not on the intentions or nature of the action itself. Quality of Pleasures: Mill distinguishes between pleasures of different qualities. He argues that intellectual and moral pleasures (higher pleasures) are more valuable than physical pleasures (lower pleasures). The preference for superior pleasures is seen as an indication of moral and intellectual development. Freedom and Autonomy: Mill defends the importance of individual freedom, as long as it does not cause harm to others. He believes that freedom of thought, expression and action are fundamental to personal and social progress. Collective Welfare: Mill's utilitarian ethics emphasizes the importance of collective welfare. Actions must seek the benefit of the majority, promoting the greatest amount of happiness possible for the greatest number of people. Justice and Rights: Although Mill recognizes the importance of justice and individual rights, he sees them as derived from the principle of utility. Justice is important because it promotes greater happiness over time. In summary, John Stuart Mill's ethics can be summarized as a search for maximizing general happiness, through the consequentialist evaluation of actions, valuing superior quality pleasures and respecting the individual freedom within the limits of collective well-being.
